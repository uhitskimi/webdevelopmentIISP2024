Artificial Intelligence is your New Coworker

Ariel Ritchey , February 6th

NEW COMPETITION

The fear that the author experiences regarding the quick advancement of artificial intelligence into the field of design is very valid. Changes in technology has almost always advanced at a gradual pace that is as a society can adjust to. Things are released in small increments through small updates, like the distance between the first computer system and implementing touchscreen abilities in 1983. Behind these updates was the excitement for something new that could change our lives for the better, as we were given the time to prepare.

This current phenomenon, however, is coming at users all around the world hard and fast. Design had started as a human artistic trait with the combination of having to conduct studies on the human mind and analyzing patterns manually. When you take that entire process away from humans, the authenticity of and connection to art is disrupted and watered down to artificially intelligence remixing things humans have already done before. I’m not that up-to-date with artificial intelligence, but to my knowledge, somebody has to physically input the already existing information for these ideas and techniques to take flight. Jobs are indeed at risk; “As Jacob Nielsen … better than you do,” is a statement that accurately acknowledges the fast occuring changes that leave designers in a “survival of the fittest” mentality, scrambling to learn how to be better than artificial intelligence and incorporate it at the same time. The reality is, humans cannot compete with such a thing that holds more data than humans our brains can comprehend. So, I think in part of allowing AI to mold and shape our lives, there will also be laws implemented to protect the well-being of humans at the same time.

IS IT USEFUL

While artificial intelligence hasn’t proved to be exactly necessary to shaping designs, it has seemingly enhanced the ability to study any given data and recognize patterns, implementing algorithms based on user’s needs. “This beneficial capability … enhancing the overall UX,” recognizes how artificial intelligence has become a useful resource for businesses and caters better to algorithms for users.

Incorporating artificial intelligence in this manner doesn’t seem all that harmful — similar to using Google for research compared to a hard copy book — allowing designers to put their skills to use and maintain authentic art and the connections people have to it. There should be a balance between the use of artificial intelligence and human authenticity so that the value of art isn’t diminished in the process of advancement.

DRAWBACKS

More on the negative impacts of artificial intelligence in art, I’ve encountered a lot of people fight back on it, even witnessed the original artists themselves find their original pieces of theirs floating around on the internet, altered slightly and untrue to their creation. This could evoke some self-doubt in an artist. I reckon over time that as the unrealistic body depictions and way too many body parts become a thing of the past, that it’ll be harder to tell what’s been artificially generated or not. Artists are now in competition with robots, knowingly or unknowingly, losing opportunities because people want cheaper and faster alternatives. Jobs could be run out of existence in the long-run and the drive to create art could be run to the ground. In addition to this, there’s always the possibility of artists who do create original art being accused of using artificial intelligence which robs them of being acknowledged for their hard work. This isn’t something that I’ve witnessed before personally from an original artist, but I don’t think it’s something that could be completely disregarded as a possibility. When it comes down to it, there seems to be some pros and cons of artificial intelligence in our lives that is here to stay.
